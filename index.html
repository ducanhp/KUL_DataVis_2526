What W.N.B.A. Players Would Earn Under a Fairer System

By Duc Anh Pham, Yueli Ni, Tiantian Qin, Xiao Zheng, Liu Luvin
A project by Group 15 - Data Visualisation Course - KU Leuven
Oct. 3, 2025

It is no secret that the WNBA is bringing in less revenue compared to its senior counterpart, the NBA. Recent years however have seen a significant rise in interest of the public towards the women’s league and hence yielded also a proportional positive return financially. The league is sure to reap the results from this positive influx while the players are not. In a leading and role model such as the WNBA, its players are still being cast on the side line and their financial rewards are so as such.

In 2024, Caitlin Clark arrived in the WNBA as one of the most anticipated prospects in the history of women's basketball. The former Iowa star shattered viewership records, sold out arenas across the country, and became the face of a new era for the league. As the No. 1 overall pick in the WNBA Draft, Clark signed a four-year rookie contract worth approximately $338,000. But just months earlier, Zaccharie Risacher was selected first overall in the NBA Draft. His four-year rookie contract was valued at approximately $57.1 million.

The difference is difficult to comprehend in absolute terms. Risacher's rookie deal is around 169 times larger than Clark's. Put another way, Clark's entire four-year contract represents just a fractional 0.0059% of Risacher's rookie earnings.

Despite being the W.N.B.A No. 1 pick, Caitlin Clark's entire rookie contract is just a fractional 0.59% of the N.B.A No. 1 pick equivalent, Zach Risacher

Sources: Spotrac WNBA, Spotrac NBA

The gap is not a reflection of individual talent or draft status, both players entered their respective leagues as the most sought-after prospects of their class. Instead, it reflects the vastly different economic structures that govern player compensation in the NBA and WNBA.

As striking as the comparison is, it only tells part of the story. The disparity extends beyond star rookies and can be observed across the leagues as a whole, where even the typical NBA player earns substantially more than their WNBA counterpart.

Caitlin Clark’s case is not just isolated but can be seen across the league, on average female basketball athletes make 10 or 20 times less than their peers in the NBA (source).

The gap between WNBA and NBA salaries becomes even more striking when viewed through the lens of time.

In 2025, the median WNBA player earns a base salary of $80,823, compared with a roughly $6.7 million paycheck for the median NBA player in the 2024–25 season. Yet players in both leagues spend a similar amount of time on the court, with median playing times of 17.8 and 19.1 minutes per game, respectively.

When salaries are translated into game time, the difference is stark. A median WNBA player needs approximately 1 minute and 29 seconds of playing time to earn $100. A median NBA player reaches the same amount in just 1.74 seconds. This is illustrated below.

Why does such a gulf exist? The answer is not that the women's game fails to make money, it is how that money is divided once it comes in. The next three sections trace the pay gap back to its structural roots: how revenue is shared, how the salary cap is set, and how the pay tiers themselves are built.

I. The revenue exists, the players just don't see it.

The first and most fundamental problem is the split. When the WNBA collects a dollar, only a sliver of it reaches the women who generate it on the court. In the NBA, players collectively take home roughly half of all basketball-related revenue. In the WNBA, that figure has historically sat at about 9.3 percent. The contrast is hard to miss. Where the NBA dollar splits down the middle between players and owners, the WNBA dollar is carved up among team owners, the NBA itself, and outside investors long before it reaches a single athlete. And this split was set when the league was small. As revenue has surged, the share formula has not moved with it, so the faster the league grows, the wider the gap between what comes in and what players receive.

II. Even willing teams can't pay more.

Suppose a team owner wanted to pay her players what they are worth. Under the current rules, she could not. Each WNBA team operates under a hard salary cap, a strict ceiling on total player pay, and that cap has been kept low even as the league's finances have improved. Revenue sharing decides how the pie is split; the salary cap decides how big the players' slice is even allowed to be. III. A ladder with too few rungs.

Money is only half the story. Even if the WNBA shared more revenue and raised its cap, players would still run into a third barrier: the pay structure itself. Salaries are sorted into a small set of rigid tiers, and a player's ceiling is decided largely by her years of service rather than how well she actually plays. The result is a ladder with too few rungs and no express lane for excellence, so a breakout star can be stuck earning the same as a benchwarmer who happens to have the same seniority.

Even setting aside the dollar gap, the W.N.B.A.'s pay ladder is shorter — and has no shortcut for performance

Each step shows the most a player can earn as a share of their own league's salary cap, so league size is stripped out. The N.B.A. lets a standout rookie skip years of waiting via the "Rose Rule." The W.N.B.A.'s old system had no such path, and topped out at less than half the N.B.A.'s ceiling.

A short ladder would matter less if pay still tracked performance within it, but it does not. When you plot what players actually earn against how effectively they play, the link is weak: plenty of highly efficient players sit well below the pay of less productive peers. Where things stand now.

Taken together, the three problems compound one another. Too little revenue is shared with players; the salary cap keeps even willing teams from paying more; and a rigid tier system caps how far any single player can climb regardless of how well she performs. None of these is about whether the women's game can draw a crowd, it plainly can. They are about the rules written around it.

Since this essay was first drafted, those rules have begun to change. In March 2026, after seventeen months of negotiation and a near-unanimous strike authorization by the players, the WNBA and the players' union ratified a new collective bargaining agreement, the first ever to include a revenue-sharing model that scales as the league grows. The headline numbers move in exactly the directions the charts above argued for. The team salary cap jumps from $1.5 million to roughly $7 million, the average salary rises from about $120,000 toward the $600,000 range, the minimum salary climbs from around $66,000 to more than $300,000, and the players' share of revenue moves from roughly 9.3 percent toward nearly 20 percent over the life of the seven-year deal.

That is real, hard-won progress, and it addresses the first two problems head-on: more money now reaches players, and a far higher cap gives teams room to pay them. But the work is not finished. A 20 percent revenue share is a major leap from 9.3 percent, yet it is still well short of the roughly 50 percent that players in the major men's leagues receive, so the gap this essay set out to measure has narrowed rather than closed. And the question of structure, of building a pay ladder with enough rungs and a genuine path to reward elite performance, remains the next frontier. The 2026 season, the league's thirtieth, will be the first test of whether betting on these athletes pays off. If the league keeps growing as it has, the case for closing the remaining distance will only get stronger.
