What W.N.B.A. Players Would Earn Under a Fairer System

By Duc Anh Pham, Yueli Ni, Tiantian Qin, Xiao Zheng, Liu Luvin
A project by Group 15 - Data Visualisation Course - KU Leuven
Oct. 3, 2025

It is no secret that the W.N.B.A. is bringing in less revenue compared to its senior counterpart, the N.B.A. Recent years however have seen a significant rise in interest of the public towards the women’s league and hence yielded also a proportional positive return financially. The league is sure to reap the results from this positive influx while the players are not. In a leading and role model such as the W.N.B.A., its players are still being cast on the side line and their financial rewards are negatively represented.

In 2024, Caitlin Clark arrived in the W.N.B.A. as one of the most anticipated prospects in the history of women's basketball. The former Iowa star shattered viewership records, sold out arenas across the country, and became the face of a new era for the league. As the No. 1 overall pick in the W.N.B.A. Draft, Clark signed a four-year rookie contract worth approximately $338,000. But just months earlier, Zaccharie Risacher was selected first overall in the N.B.A. Draft. His four-year rookie contract was valued at approximately $57.1 millions.

The difference is difficult to comprehend in absolute terms. Risacher's rookie deal is around 169 times larger than Clark's. Put another way, Clark's entire four-year contract represents just a fractional 0.59% of Risacher's rookie earnings.

Despite being the W.N.B.A No. 1 pick, Caitlin Clark's entire rookie contract is just a fractional 0.59% of the N.B.A No. 1 pick equivalent, Zach Risacher

While this initial comparison is striking, the financial gap is not driven by a difference in on-court performance. Both athletes arrived as generational talents expected to redefine their teams. Instead, their compensation is entirely dictated by the divergent revenue models and economic realities of the N.B.A. versus the W.N.B.A. Yet, the story extends far beyond the draft lottery and Caitlin Clark’s case. A broader look at the leagues reveals a systemic disparity that impacts every roster where W.N.B.A. players are compensated significantly lower than their N.B.A. counterparts.

This is evident when looking at what a typical W.N.B.A. player earns in the 2025 season. During that season, the median base salary for a female athlete is only $80,823 compared to that of a roughly $6.7 million paycheck for the median NBA player in the 2024–25 season. Yet players in both leagues spend a rather similar amount of time on the court, with median playing times of 17.8 and 19.1 minutes per game, respectively. However, a typical W.N.B.A. player needs approximately 53 seconds of playing time to earn $100 while a typical N.B.A. player reaches the same amount in just 1.74 seconds.

It takes a typical W.N.B.A. player significantly longer on the court to earn $100 compared to that of a N.B.A. player

Sources: Spotrac WNBA, Spotrac NBA.

Why does such a massive divide exist? The easiest assumption people would make is that the women’s game simply doesn't generate the revenue required to pay its athletes more and hence the players should be happy with what they have. But the real answer isn't about a lack of incoming capital, it is about how those dollars are carved up once they arrive. The pay gap is a deliberate product of institutional inefficiencies and unfair representations. To understand why it persists, we have to trace the money back to three structural but overarching pillars: how revenue is shared, how the salary cap is set, and how the pay tiers themselves are built.

The revenue exists, the players just don't see it

The first and most fundamental problem is the revenue split. Every season, both leagues decide how much money from the revenue pool should go to all players.

When the W.N.B.A. collects a dollar, only a fraction of it reaches the women who generate it on the court. During the 2025 season, for every $100 of W.N.B.A. revenue, the player share historically sat at just $9.30. The remaining funds are heavily fractured: $42 goes to W.N.B.A. owners, $42 is diverted to NBA owners, and $16 is allocated to outside investors. Even within the W.N.B.A. owners' share, $32.70 is absorbed by operations and other costs before the players see their cut.

In stark contrast, the N.B.A. operates on a much more straightforward and equitable model. Since the league itself is composed of only N.B.A. franchises owners, the revenue is directly shared between the players and the owners. For the 2024-25 season, every $100 of revenue is split evenly right down the middle. N.B.A. players collectively take home $50, while the other $50 goes to N.B.A. owners. From that owners' half, $40 covers team operations, leaving $10 for profit and reserves.

For the patient observer of the W.N.B.A.’s historic economic rise, a troubling trend emerges beneath the top-line success. While the league celebrates surging viewership, lucrative new media rights, and sold-out arenas, the players’ share of that expanding financial pie has essentially stagnated. The traditional argument urges athletes to wait, insisting that a rising tide will eventually lift their salaries. However, the data reveals a fundamental structural disconnect between the league's rapid revenue growth and actual player compensation.

This raises a critical question: what would the financial landscape look like if the W.N.B.A. adopted the equitable revenue-sharing models considered standard in other major professional sports?

To show just how big this gap really is, we calculated what would happen if players got exactly half of the money they help bring in, a true 50-50 split. When you compare that fair share to the roughly 9.3 percent they actually get today, the difference is shocking. As the chart below shows, the league's total revenue is shooting up every year, but the amount spent on player salaries is barely moving. It paints a clear picture: the league is making more money than ever, but the athletes who make it all happen are missing out.

A common misconception surrounding revenue-sharing debates is that a structural financial overhaul would primarily serve to enrich the league's most visible superstars. However, implementing an equitable 50-50 split, mirroring the standard long established in the N.B.A., would trigger a foundational shift across the entirety of the wage scale. The economic impact of this adjustment would be universal, cascading down from franchise centerpieces to the final spot on the bench.

For the league’s elite, maximum contracts would finally expand to reflect their true market value as the primary drivers of viewership and merchandise. Yet, the most profound structural changes would occur further down the roster. The average player, the rotational athletes who form the competitive backbone of every franchise, would also see their compensation transformed from a modest baseline into genuine, career-sustaining wealth.

Crucially, this rising tide would also lift the athletes occupying the lowest rungs of the pay scale. A proportional revenue split would drastically elevate the league minimum, offering immediate financial stability to the most vulnerable players and fundamentally altering the trajectory of their careers. Current salary cap means that even willing teams can't pay more to their players

Suppose a team owner wanted to pay her players what they are worth. Under the current rules, she could not. Each WNBA team operates under a hard salary cap, a strict ceiling on total player pay, and that cap has been kept low even as the league's finances have improved. Revenue sharing decides how the pie is split; the salary cap decides how big the players' slice is even allowed to be.

On the surface, the difference in team spending for the 2025 season is staggering: the N.B.A. gave its 30 teams a salary cap of $140.588 million each, while the 13 W.N.B.A. teams were capped at just $1,507,100. But the real story is told by how much of the pie each league actually shares.

The N.B.A. makes roughly $12.25 billion a year and dedicates 34.4% of it to its total salary cap. The W.N.B.A. brings in $300 million, yet only 5.02% of that money goes toward team caps. This raises a compelling question: what if the W.N.B.A. adopted a similar financial structure?

If the league applied the N.B.A.'s 34.4% sharing model to its $300 million revenue, the total pool for player salaries would jump to roughly $103 million. Divided evenly across the 13 franchises, each team's salary cap would rise to almost $7.9 million. Compared to the current $1.5 million limit, that is a 5.3 multiple, giving teams the financial flexibility to finally pay their athletes a fair market share.

Playing better doesn't mean earning more

Money is only half the story. Even if the WNBA shared more revenue and raised its cap, players would still run into a third barrier: the pay structure itself. Salaries are sorted into a small set of rigid tiers, and a player's ceiling is decided largely by her years of service rather than how well she actually plays. The result is a ladder with too few rungs and no express lane for excellence, so a breakout star can be stuck earning the same as a benchwarmer who happens to have the same seniority.

When we look closely at how much players get paid compared to how well they actually play, it is easy to see that the current system is broken. Right now, a small group of players on the biggest contracts take up almost all of the money. At the same time, the rules of the league put a strict limit on everyone else. Many players are stuck in lower-paying contracts even when they play amazingly well on the court. The system simply does not allow them to be paid what they are truly worth based on their talent.

You can see this problem clearly in the graph below. Out of the 182 players in the league, 35 of them are stuck in the bottom-right section of the chart. This spot on the graph represents players who perform at an incredibly high level but are still getting paid way below average.

When nearly one out of every five players is this underpaid despite playing great basketball, we have to ask: what would happen if they simply got a fair deal based on how good they actually are?

To figure this out, we created a new system that throws away the old pay limits. Instead, we based player pay entirely on a common basketball stat called the Player Efficiency Rating, or PER for short. This stat measures how much of a positive impact a player has on the game.

In our new setup, the rules are simple and fair. If a player earns a PER score between 13 and 20, they automatically get a solid, mid-level contract. If a player steps up their game and scores between 20 and 25, they earn a standard maximum contract. Finally, the very best players in the league who score above 25 are immediately rewarded with a supermax contract, which is the highest pay available.

By getting rid of the old limits and tying pay directly to performance, the league could become much more fair. It is a simple change that makes sure players are finally rewarded for exactly what matters most: how well they play the game of basketball.

Where things stand now.

Taken together, the three problems compound one another. Too little revenue is shared with players; the salary cap keeps even willing teams from paying more; and a rigid tier system caps how far any single player can climb regardless of how well she performs. None of these is about whether the women's game can draw a crowd, it plainly can. They are about the rules written around it.

Since this essay was first drafted, those rules have begun to change. In March 2026, after seventeen months of negotiation and a near-unanimous strike authorization by the players, the WNBA and the players' union ratified a new collective bargaining agreement, the first ever to include a revenue-sharing model that scales as the league grows. The headline numbers move in exactly the directions the charts above argued for. The team salary cap jumps from $1.5 million to roughly $7 million, the average salary rises from about $120,000 toward the $600,000 range, the minimum salary climbs from around $66,000 to more than $300,000, and the players' share of revenue moves from roughly 9.3 percent toward nearly 20 percent over the life of the seven-year deal.

That is real, hard-won progress, and it addresses the first two problems head-on: more money now reaches players, and a far higher cap gives teams room to pay them. But the work is not finished. A 20 percent revenue share is a major leap from 9.3 percent, yet it is still well short of the roughly 50 percent that players in the major men's leagues receive, so the gap this essay set out to measure has narrowed rather than closed. And the question of structure, of building a pay ladder with enough rungs and a genuine path to reward elite performance, remains the next frontier. The 2026 season, the league's thirtieth, will be the first test of whether betting on these athletes pays off. If the league keeps growing as it has, the case for closing the remaining distance will only get stronger.
