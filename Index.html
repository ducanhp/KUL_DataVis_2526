What W.N.B.A. Players Would Earn Under a Fairer System

By Duc Anh Pham, Yueli Ni, Tiantian Quin, Xiao Zheng, Liu Luvin
A project by Group 15 - Data Visualisation Course - KU Leuven
Oct. 3, 2025

It is no secret that the WNBA is bringing in less revenue compared to its senior counterpart, the NBA. Recent years however have seen a significant rise in interest of the public towards the women’s league and hence yielded also a proportional positive return financially. The league is sure to reap the results from this positive influx while the players are not. In a leading and role model such as the WNBA, its players are still being cast on the side line and their financial rewards are so as such.

In 2024, Caitlin Clark arrived in the WNBA as one of the most anticipated prospects in the history of women's basketball. The former Iowa star shattered viewership records, sold out arenas across the country, and became the face of a new era for the league. As the No. 1 overall pick in the WNBA Draft, Clark signed a four-year rookie contract worth approximately $338,000. But just months earlier, Zaccharie Risacher was selected first overall in the NBA Draft. His four-year rookie contract was valued at approximately $57.1 million.

The difference is difficult to comprehend in absolute terms. Risacher's rookie deal is around 169 times larger than Clark's. Put another way, Clark's entire four-year contract represents just a fractional 0.0059% of Risacher's rookie earnings.

The gap is not a reflection of individual talent or draft status, both players entered their respective leagues as the most sought-after prospects of their class. Instead, it reflects the vastly different economic structures that govern player compensation in the NBA and WNBA.

As striking as the comparison is, it only tells part of the story. The disparity extends beyond star rookies and can be observed across the leagues as a whole, where even the typical NBA player earns substantially more than their WNBA counterpart.

Caitlin Clark’s case is not just isolated but can be seen across the league, on average female basketball athletes make 10 or 20 times less than their peers in the NBA (source).

The gap between WNBA and NBA salaries becomes even more striking when viewed through the lens of time.

In 2025, the median WNBA player earns a base salary of $72,455, compared with a roughly $6.7 million paycheck for the median NBA player in the 2024–25 season. Yet players in both leagues spend a similar amount of time on the court, with median playing times of 17.8 and 19.1 minutes per game, respectively.

When salaries are translated into game time, the difference is stark. A median WNBA player needs approximately 1 minute and 29 seconds of playing time to earn $100. A median NBA player reaches the same amount in just 1.74 seconds. This is illustrated below.
